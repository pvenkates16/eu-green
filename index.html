The Regulatory Toolkit: Measuring "Green"

To prevent greenwashing and guide investment, the EU created the Taxonomy Regulation—a classification system to define environmentally sustainable activities. Banks use metrics like the Green Asset Ratio (GAR) to report their alignment.

What Makes an Activity "EU Taxonomy-Aligned"?

1

Substantial Contribution

The activity must significantly contribute to one of six environmental objectives, like climate change mitigation.

2

Do No Significant Harm (DNSH)

It must not cause significant harm to any of the other five environmental objectives.

3

Minimum Safeguards

The activity must be carried out in line with human and labour rights standards.

The Reality: Low Overall Alignment

Despite the framework, the average Green Asset Ratio (GAR) for EU banks remains low, highlighting the scale of the transition challenge. This is due to strict criteria and significant data gaps, especially for smaller companies.

Bank Performance: Leaders in Transition

While overall alignment is low, several banks are making significant strides, demonstrating leadership through higher green asset ratios, substantial sustainable finance commitments, and clear fossil fuel exit strategies.

Green Asset Ratio (GAR) Comparison (2024 Data)

The GAR shows the percentage of a bank's assets aligned with the EU Taxonomy. While still in single digits, some banks report higher ratios, often influenced by large mortgage portfolios which are easier to classify as "green."

Fossil Fuel Divestment & Policy

A key indicator of commitment is a bank's policy on fossil fuels. Leaders have set clear deadlines to exit or have near-zero exposure.

  • ✔Triodos Bank: Explicit policy of no fossil fuel financing.
  • ✔La Banque Postale: Near-complete exit (0.005% exposure).
  • ✔EIB: Ended all unabated fossil fuel financing in 2021.
  • ~ING: Targets zero upstream oil & gas finance by 2040.
  • ~Crédit Agricole: Exiting thermal coal by 2030/2040 but still finances some fossil fuel producers via "green-labeled" deals.
  • ✖Nordea: Reportedly lowered sustainability requirements, allowing new loans for unconventional fossil fuel extraction and lent SEK 15 billion to fossil fuel companies.

Banks' Role: Fueling the Green Transition

Banks are pivotal in accelerating the shift from fossil fuels to green energy by deploying various financial instruments and directing capital to key participants across the economy. Their role extends beyond simple lending to encompass advisory services and innovative financing solutions.

Key Financial Means Used by Banks

Banks leverage a diverse set of financial instruments to support the energy transition:

  • Green Loans: Loans specifically earmarked for environmentally friendly projects (e.g., renewable energy installations, energy-efficient building upgrades).
  • Green Bonds: Debt instruments where the proceeds are exclusively used to finance or re-finance eligible green projects.
  • Sustainability-Linked Loans (SLLs): Loans where the interest rate is tied to the borrower's achievement of pre-defined sustainability performance targets.
  • Equity Investments: Direct investments in green technology companies, renewable energy developers, or sustainable infrastructure funds.
  • Advisory Services: Guiding clients on their decarbonization strategies, helping them identify green investment opportunities, and navigate regulatory frameworks.
  • Trade Finance: Supporting the trade of green goods and services, such as renewable energy components.

Company Spotlight: Energy Sector Leaders & Banks

Energy companies like Equinor and Ørsted exemplify the complex transition, with banks playing a crucial role in funding their diverse portfolios. Nordea and Danske Bank also demonstrate varied approaches to financing both traditional and green energy projects.

Equinor: Transitioning Energy Giant

Equinor, a Norwegian state-owned energy company, is optimizing its oil and gas portfolio while significantly growing its renewable energy and low-carbon solutions segments. It aims for net-zero emissions by 2050.

Fossil Fuel Projects & Funding:

  • **Major Oil & Gas Fields:** Rosebank (North Sea), Wisting (Arctic). These are significant new developments.
  • **Key Financiers (Illustrative/General):**
    • Barclays (e.g., bond underwriting)
    • JPMorgan Chase (e.g., bond underwriting)
    • BNP Paribas (e.g., general financing, revolving credit facilities)
    • Crédit Agricole (e.g., general financing)
    • DNB (e.g., revolving credit facility, share ownership)
    • Citi, Deutsche Bank, Goldman Sachs (e.g., bond underwriting)
  • **Note:** Many major international banks have provided general corporate financing to Equinor, including revolving credit facilities and bond underwriting, some of which indirectly support fossil fuel operations. Specific project-level financing details for fossil fuels are often not publicly disaggregated.

Green Energy & Low-Carbon Projects & Funding:

  • **Offshore Wind:** Dogger Bank (UK - world's largest), Bałtyk 2 & 3 (Poland).
  • **Floating Wind:** Hywind Tampen (Norway - powering oil & gas installations).
  • **Carbon Capture & Storage (CCS):** Northern Lights (Norway - world's first commercial third-party CO2 storage).
  • **Key Financiers (Illustrative/General):**
    • European Investment Bank (EIB) (e.g., Bałtyk projects)
    • Nordic Investment Bank (NIB) (e.g., Bałtyk projects)
    • Around 30 Polish and international financial institutions for Bałtyk projects.
    • Various commercial banks involved in general corporate financing, some of which support renewable energy growth.

Ørsted: Green Energy Pioneer

Ørsted, a Danish company, has transformed from a fossil fuel utility (DONG Energy) into the world's largest developer of offshore wind power. It aims for net-zero generation by 2025 and no carbon emissions by 2040.

Fossil Fuel Legacy & Funding:

  • **Historical:** Formerly DONG Energy, involved in Danish North Sea oil and gas. Sold off its oil and gas business to Ineos in 2017. Phased out coal for power generation.
  • **Current:** Minimal to no direct fossil fuel projects. Focus is almost entirely on renewables.
  • **Note:** Any historical financing for fossil fuels would predate its major green transformation and divestment.

Green Energy Projects & Funding:

  • **Offshore Wind:** Hornsea 1 & 2 (UK - world's largest), Greater Changhua 2a & 2b (Taiwan), Block Island Wind Farm (US).
  • **Onshore Wind & Solar:** Various projects in US, Europe.
  • **Power-to-X (e-methanol):** FlagshipONE (Sweden - pioneering e-methanol production).
  • **Key Financiers (Illustrative/General):**
    • A consortium of 25 international and local banks for Greater Changhua 2 (e.g., for project financing).
    • Export Credit Agencies (ECAs): Export Finance Norway (Eksfin), EIFO Denmark, KEXIM (South Korea), T-EXIM (Taiwan), UK Export Finance (UKEF).
    • European Investment Bank (EIB) (e.g., FlagshipONE via EU-Catalyst partnership).
    • Pension Funds (e.g., Goldman Sachs, ATP, PFA Pension for equity stakes in projects).
  • **Note:** Ørsted's funding for green projects often involves large consortia of banks and ECAs for project-specific financing, in addition to group-level corporate funding.

Nordea: Navigating the Transition

Nordea, a leading Nordic bank, aims for net-zero emissions by 2050 and has reduced financed emissions in its oil and gas portfolio. However, its recent lending policy changes have drawn criticism regarding continued fossil fuel support.

Fossil Fuel Projects & Funding:

  • **Lending Policy Shift:** Recently adopted a more lenient policy, allowing new loans to companies involved in oil sands, shale oil, and Arctic extraction.
  • **Motivation:** Nordea has defended this policy change by arguing that new sources of oil and gas are needed for Europe's energy security, stating a social responsibility to help secure Europe's energy supply.
  • **Specific Lending:** Provided a SEK 2.4 billion loan to Equinor for shale oil/gas (Vaca Muerta, Argentina) which was controversial. Reportedly lent SEK 15 billion to fossil fuel companies in the last two years.
  • **Reduced Exposure (Claimed):** States oil and gas lending is less than 0.1% of total lending, with a 92% reduction since 2019 in lending volume and 74% in financed emissions from the sector.
  • **Key Financiers (General):** As a major Nordic bank, its general corporate financing and bond underwriting activities would involve a broad range of international banks, though specific project-level fossil fuel financing details are limited.
  • **Note:** While Nordea maintains it does not provide *project financing* for unconventional or Arctic exploration, critics argue that general corporate loans can indirectly support such activities.

Green Energy & Sustainable Finance:

  • **Net-Zero Target:** Aims for net-zero emissions by 2050, with a 40-50% reduction in emissions across investment and lending portfolios by 2030.
  • **Sustainable Finance Facilitation:** Target of >€200 billion in sustainable financing facilitated between 2022-2025.
  • **Products:** Offers Green Bonds and Sustainability-Linked Loans (SLLs) to finance renewable energy, energy efficiency, clean transportation, and green buildings.
  • **Project Example:** Provided a €1.0 million EU-guaranteed loan to Liftra ApS, a Danish equipment supplier for the wind turbine industry.
  • **Key Financiers (General):** Nordea itself acts as a financier, and its sustainable funding frameworks are supported by various investors in green bonds and SLLs.

Danske Bank: Committed to Transition

Danske Bank, a major Nordic bank, has implemented a policy to restrict financing for oil and gas upstream expansion and is a leading provider of green loans and sustainable finance products.

Fossil Fuel Projects & Funding:

  • **Exclusion Policy:** Excludes general finance, project finance, and investment services for upstream oil and gas expansion (defined as expanding supply beyond Dec 31, 2021 levels).
  • **Coal Exclusion:** Excludes companies with >5% revenue from thermal coal mining or coal/peat-fired power generation.
  • **"Ring-fenced" Loophole & Motivation:** Allows financing for renewable energy or CCUS activities *within* fossil fuel companies. Critics argue this can indirectly free up capital for fossil fuel activities, despite the bank's commitment to supporting an orderly transition to low-carbon economies.
  • **Reduced Exposure:** Reduced fossil investments by 37% and lending to oil and gas companies by 50% since 2020.
  • **Key Financiers (General):** Danske Bank's own policies restrict its financing of fossil fuels, but its broader corporate funding would involve a range of international banks.

Green Energy & Sustainable Finance:

  • **Net-Zero Target:** Climate Action Plan aims for net-zero emissions, with targets for reducing emissions intensity in oil and gas, energy utilities, shipping, commercial real estate, and mortgages.
  • **Green Lending Growth:** Green loan volume grew significantly from DKK 37.5 billion (2021) to DKK 56.3 billion (end 2022).
  • **Key Green Sectors:** Largest share of green loans for green buildings and renewable energy projects (e.g., wind power parks, biofuel refineries).
  • **Specific Projects:** Provided €53 million debt financing for a 100 MW solar PV farm in Sweden (Neoen and Alight). Acted as Joint Bookrunner on Ørsted's €750m green hybrid capital bond issue.
  • **Products:** Leading Nordic arranger of Green Bonds, Sustainable Bonds, and Sustainability-Linked Loans.
  • **Key Financiers (General):** Danske Bank itself is a major financier in sustainable projects, and its green bonds are purchased by various investors.

The Credibility Gap: Pledges vs. Reality

A critical challenge is the divergence between banks' public climate commitments and their continued financing of fossil fuel activities. Reports show many large banks still support fossil fuel expansion, raising concerns about the pace of genuine change.
